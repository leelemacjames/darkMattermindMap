On the Misplaced Omnipotence of Light

Poincaré catalogued the theories of his contemporaries and found, beneath the variety, a common grammar: matter is where the substrate does something singular. A knot. A vortex. A destruction. A twist. We have replaced the ether with quantum fields, the vortices with gauge bosons, the torsion with curvature of spacetime. But the grammar is unchanged. Matter remains, for us, the locus of the special.

Einstein gave us two equivalences. The first, E = mc², was derived from the electrodynamics of moving bodies. Its derivation assumes Lorentz covariance, which is to say, it assumes that the speed of light is the invariant speed. The second, m_inertial = m_gravitational, was elevated from an empirical coincidence of Newtonian mechanics — measured with pendulums, torsion balances, falling bodies. All baryonic. All luminous. All coupled to the photon.

We then applied both equivalences to dark matter without hesitation. Dark matter follows geodesics. Dark matter has a rest-mass energy. Dark matter satisfies E² = p²c² + m²c⁴. But the Lorentz invariance from which this dispersion relation derives was established by studying electromagnetic phenomena. The equivalence principle was tested with electromagnetic matter. We have no independent confirmation that a substance blind to photons must respect the geometric structure that photons revealed to us.

This is not to say the equivalences fail for dark matter. They may hold perfectly. But we should be honest that we are assuming they hold, and that this assumption is grounded in a theoretical framework — general relativity — that was itself constructed from the behaviour of the 5% of the universe we can see. The free-falling g-field exists independent of whether there is a corporeal test mass to fall under its potential. But does dark matter fall? Or does it do something else entirely in the presence of curvature?

As anti-entropic baryonic creatures, indebted to our illuminating photon source, we are perhaps guilty of assigning to light a misplaced omnipotence. The bookkeeper quantifies. The bookkeeper's tools are photons. What escapes the count may also escape the rules we wrote while counting.
